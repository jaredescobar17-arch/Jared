---

Here's the complete process I use to publish high-quality AI content on the Ahrefs blog.

There are parts of my job I don't like: writing my 500th article on content gap analysis, listing the features of 30 free SEO tools for yet another listicle, updating old content because a competitor pushed me out of the top three. These are necessary hygiene tasks. I'd rather spend time publishing original research or contrarian opinions. So I use AI to speed up the tedious processes — condensing several days of research, writing and revision into a couple of hours. Despite containing zero words written by a human, these articles perform as well as human-written content.

## Important caveats

- **This still requires skill and effort.** It's a tool used by a skilled writer, not a replacement for one. Hands-off automation will disappoint you.
- **90% of success comes from good topic selection.** Works well for simple informational topics where I have passing knowledge (so I can tell if it's good). I do NOT use it for opinion pieces, research content, or new/rapidly changing topics.
- **It's not a perfect process.** It's messy and meant to be tailored.

## 1. Create Markdown files of our existing writing processes
I broke down the Ahrefs editorial process into specific, manageable steps and documented them in Markdown an LLM can follow: topic selection, briefing, outlining, structural editing, drafting, incorporating Ahrefs products, line editing, internal links, metadata, and WordPress shortcodes. Each doc has simple guidelines + examples, drawn from our team docs, favorite blog posts about good writing (condensed by ChatGPT), excerpts from my writing courses, and real writing samples.

## 2. Create a ChatGPT project and upload the process documents
Projects let you upload reference docs and set custom instructions that apply to every conversation. The custom instructions cast the model as "a senior content strategist at Ahrefs… writing under the editorial direction of Ryan Law," tell it the audience is "savvy marketers and SEOs who are skeptical of fluff," and instruct it to follow the documented workflow in order. The most crucial "Pareto prompts": (1) always consult the project documentation and work in sequence; (2) a summary of the target audience; (3) roleplay as someone with "in-the-trenches experience" (responsible for first-person anecdotes/examples).

## 3. Deep research
For some topics I run a deep research request to source extra info (e.g., whether LLM providers endorsed llms.txt), then incorporate an AI-generated summary into the brief.

## 4. Frontload key points with a simple content brief
I front-load all human input at the start, then let AI run. The brief contains: target keyword + on-page directions; working title (to match search intent); key points to include (anecdotes, deep-research findings, unique angles); subtopics to cover (from Ahrefs' AI Content Helper analyzing the SERP); and Ahrefs products to mention.

## 5. Read, review, nudge
I become an editor. ChatGPT generates a bullet outline (H2s, BLUF summary per section, nested supporting points). I request structural changes, then move to drafting in ChatGPT Canvas so I can leave inline comments like: "too vague, cut weasel words," "include a real example," "correct this wrong idea," "trim/expand," "make it beginner-friendly." Because structure was approved at the outline stage, it gets publish-ready quickly. (He notes Claude is reputedly better for long-form, but Canvas is why he uses ChatGPT.)

## 6. Generate internal links, metadata, and WordPress shortcodes
Final stages: create metadata (also used as homepage preview text); insert WordPress shortcodes per documentation; generate ~10 internal links (ChatGPT hallucinates URLs, so he plans to feed it a real URL list; Site Audit catches broken links). Images are still added manually.

## 7. Monitor performance
Ahrefs' research found the correlation between AI content use and ranking position is 0.011 — effectively zero; AI content can and does rank. He tracks AI-generated articles in Ahrefs Portfolios and Web Analytics (one llms.txt guide earned 17,200 pageviews, traffic from social, email, and AI assistants like ChatGPT and Gemini).

## Final thoughts
"This is not a magic, zero-effort process… I still read, review, edit, and approve every piece of content published on the Ahrefs blog, whether it was written by a person or generated by ChatGPT. Using AI is no excuse for publishing shoddy content."
